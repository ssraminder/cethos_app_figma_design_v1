> **Why this training exists.** SOP VM-001 §5.7 requires every linguist to complete this module **and pass its knowledge check (≥80%)** *before* being assigned to any COA project step. Linguistic validation of a Clinical Outcome Assessment is not ordinary translation: the output is a measurement instrument used in regulated clinical trials, and a conceptually inaccurate item can invalidate trial data. This module establishes the shared methodology, documentation discipline, and confidentiality obligations the work demands.

---

## 1. Purpose

To ensure that every linguist working on a COA understands and applies the standardized, internationally recognized linguistic-validation (LV) methodology, produces a **conceptually equivalent** and **culturally appropriate** instrument, and maintains the audit trail and confidentiality controls required for regulated clinical work.

## 2. Scope

**Applies to:** translators, reconcilers, back-translators, reviewers, harmonizers, and cognitive-debriefing consultants assigned to COA / PRO / ClinRO / ObsRO / PerfO instruments.

**Does not apply to:** general (non-clinical) certified translation, which is governed by SOP-001/VM-002. A linguist may hold a general qualification yet still require this module before COA assignment.

## 3. Learning objectives

On completion, the linguist can:
1. Explain the goal of COA linguistic validation — **measurement equivalence**, not literal translation.
2. Name and sequence the steps of the ISPOR/FDA LV workflow and state the purpose of each.
3. Distinguish conceptual equivalence from literal equivalence and apply decentering.
4. Document translation rationale and item-level decisions to an auditable standard.
5. Conduct or interpret cognitive debriefing with the target population.
6. Apply Cethos's confidentiality and data-handling rules for clinical materials.

## 4. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **COA** | Clinical Outcome Assessment — a measure of how a patient feels, functions, or survives. Includes PRO, ClinRO, ObsRO, PerfO. |
| **PRO** | Patient-Reported Outcome — reported directly by the patient without interpretation by a clinician. |
| **Conceptual equivalence** | The translated item measures the **same underlying concept** as the source, in language natural to the target population — *not* a word-for-word rendering. |
| **Decentering** | Translating so that source and target are equally valid expressions of the concept, rather than forcing the target to mirror source phrasing. |
| **Reconciliation** | Merging two independent forward translations into one agreed version. |
| **Cognitive debriefing (CD)** | Testing the draft translation with target-population respondents to confirm comprehension and relevance. |
| **Harmonization** | Cross-language review ensuring all language versions of a study capture concepts consistently. |
| **Source / developer query** | A documented question to the instrument developer/sponsor when a concept is ambiguous or untranslatable. |

## 5. Module content

### 5.1 The goal: measurement equivalence
A COA is a **scientific instrument**. If the German and the Spanish versions of an item do not elicit the same concept, the trial cannot pool or compare their data. The linguist's job is to preserve the **construct being measured**, the **response options**, the **recall period**, and the **reading level** — not to produce elegant prose. Literal accuracy that changes the concept is a failure; a natural rendering that preserves the concept is success.

### 5.2 The standardized LV workflow (ISPOR / FDA)
Cethos follows the ISPOR Principles of Good Practice (Wild et al., 2005). The controlled steps, each producing a documented record:

1. **Preparation** — obtain the source, concept-elaboration/annotations, and developer guidance; raise initial queries.
2. **Forward translation (×2, independent)** — two qualified in-country native translators produce independent forward versions, each with rationale notes.
3. **Reconciliation** — a third linguist reconciles the two into a single forward version, documenting each choice.
4. **Back translation** — an independent translator (blind to the source) back-translates the reconciled version into the source language.
5. **Back-translation review** — compare back translation to source; flag and resolve conceptual discrepancies; raise developer queries as needed.
6. **Harmonization** — review across languages in the same study for consistent concept capture; resolve cross-language issues.
7. **Cognitive debriefing** — test the draft with **5–8 target-population respondents**; capture comprehension problems and alternative wording.
8. **Review of CD results & finalization** — incorporate findings; finalize the translation.
9. **Proofreading** — final independent linguistic/typographic check.
10. **Final LV report** — the complete documented trail (translations, reconciliation rationale, back translation, queries/resolutions, CD report, final version).

> No step may be skipped or merged without a documented, sponsor-approved deviation. The **audit trail is the deliverable** as much as the translation is.

### 5.3 Conceptual equivalence in practice
- Translate the **concept and the patient experience**, using terms a layperson in the target population actually uses.
- Preserve **response scales** (e.g. "not at all / a little / quite a bit / very much") so spacing and intensity match.
- Preserve the **recall period** ("in the past 7 days") exactly.
- Match the **reading level** (typically ≤ grade 6–8); do not elevate register.
- When a concept does not exist in the target culture, **raise a developer query** — never silently substitute.
- Apply **decentering**: prefer wording that is equally natural in source and target rather than calquing the source.

### 5.4 Documentation discipline
Every item-level decision that is not trivial must be **traceable**: what was chosen, why, and any alternative considered. Reconcilers and reviewers record rationale; back-translation discrepancies and their resolutions are logged; developer queries and answers are retained. This is what makes the work reproducible and inspection-ready (ISO 17100 reproducibility; 21 CFR Part 11 record integrity).

### 5.5 Cognitive debriefing essentials
- Recruit **5–8 respondents** representative of the trial population (condition, age range, literacy).
- Use **retrospective and/or think-aloud** probing to test comprehension, not preference.
- Record verbatim comprehension difficulties; propose and justify wording changes.
- The CD report is part of the final LV deliverable.

### 5.6 Confidentiality & data handling (clinical materials)
- An **NDA must be active** before any clinical material is shared (clickwrap bound to the linguist record).
- Handle instruments as **confidential, often pre-publication, sponsor-owned** material: secure transfer only; no storage on personal/unsecured services; **no public machine-translation or unauthorized AI tools** on clinical content.
- **Delete** working copies on project completion per the client/sponsor retention instruction.
- Personal/patient data encountered in CD is handled under GCP and applicable privacy law; never retained beyond need.

### 5.7 Common failure modes (and the rule)
| Failure | Rule |
|---|---|
| Literal back-translatable wording that loses the concept | Translate the **concept**, confirm via back translation |
| "Improving" or shortening the item | **Do not** edit the instrument's content/intent |
| Changing the recall period or response options | Preserve **exactly** |
| Silent substitution of an untranslatable concept | **Raise a developer query** |
| Skipping rationale notes | Every non-trivial choice is **documented** |
| Using public MT on the instrument | **Prohibited** for confidential clinical content |

## 6. Knowledge check (trainee copy)

Pass mark **≥ 80% (≥ 12 / 15)**. Closed-book. A failing attempt requires re-study and re-test; the linguist is **not assignable to COA work** until a pass is recorded.

1. The primary goal of COA linguistic validation is to produce a translation that is…
   a) word-for-word identical to the source  b) **conceptually equivalent and culturally appropriate**  c) shorter and simpler than the source  d) in formal literary register
2. How many independent forward translations are produced in the standard ISPOR workflow? a) one  b) **two**  c) four  d) as many as time allows
3. Reconciliation means… a) back-translating  b) **merging the two forward translations into one agreed version**  c) testing with patients  d) proofreading
4. The purpose of back translation is to… a) improve style  b) **check conceptual equivalence against the source**  c) replace forward translation  d) shorten the instrument
5. Cognitive debriefing is normally conducted with how many target-population respondents? a) 1–2  b) **5–8**  c) 20–30  d) none
6. A concept in the source has no direct equivalent in the target culture. You should… a) omit it  b) pick the closest word silently  c) **raise a developer/sponsor query**  d) translate literally
7. The recall period ("in the past 7 days") should be… a) localized to a round number  b) **preserved exactly**  c) removed  d) left to the patient
8. Response options ("not at all / a little / quite a bit / very much") must… a) be alphabetized  b) **preserve the intensity and spacing of the source scale**  c) be reduced to yes/no  d) be expanded
9. "Decentering" means… a) centering text on the page  b) **wording that is equally natural in source and target, not a calque**  c) removing the patient's perspective  d) ignoring the source
10. Which is part of the **final LV deliverable**? a) only the final translation  b) **the full documented trail incl. reconciliation rationale, back translation, queries, and CD report**  c) an invoice  d) the translator's CV
11. Before any clinical instrument is shared with you, there must be… a) a purchase order  b) **an active NDA bound to your record**  c) a public glossary  d) nothing
12. Using a public/free machine-translation tool on a confidential COA instrument is… a) encouraged for speed  b) fine if you delete it after  c) **prohibited**  d) allowed for back translation only
13. The target reading level of most COA items is approximately… a) university  b) **grade 6–8 / lay reader**  c) clinician-only  d) irrelevant
14. If you make a non-obvious item-level translation choice, you must… a) keep it to yourself  b) **document the rationale**  c) ask the patient  d) change it back later
15. Harmonization ensures… a) identical fonts  b) **consistent concept capture across the study's languages**  c) faster delivery  d) lower cost

## 7. Completion & records

- A completion is recorded **per linguist** in `qms.competence_evidence` (evidence type *COA methodology training*) with the module version, date, and knowledge-check score, **verified by the Quality Manager / MD**.
- A linguist with no current passing record is **not eligible** for COA step assignment (the assignment gate, VM-001 §5.7).
- Re-training on a material methodology or regulatory change, otherwise at re-qualification.
- Records retained ≥ 5 years.
